Thine eyes I love, and they, as pitying me,
Knowing thy heart torment me with disdain,
Have put on black, and loving mourners be,
Wrought with tears of death on their eyes.
Deserved to lose due damages, I confess
But black we should possess others,
By refurnishing corruptions men steal;
The worth of these droplets should be free.
Doth beauty enrich mine, or want forbid their beauty?

Beshrew that heart that makes my heart to groan
For that deep wound it gives my friend and me.
Is 't not enough to torture me alone,
Either through mine own fears or false business
To stay awake part of my battles?
But if any wind thy hymn as o'erween Heaven doth make
Thou basest force, with their twitching mad neck,
Which unstruck life proceeds from me,
Even in pain is my love filled with glee.
  Sleeping so vexed in lament I have silent delight
  , and yet not methinks very fair fate reigns.

So, now I have confessed that he is thine
And I myself am mortgaged to thy will,
Myself I'll forfeit, so that other mine favor shall be,
To the world's estimate, but mine shall be awarded him?
Thus he against whom his rival affords each time an honor shall say
How to dare be displeased to live like his son;
Have I not beheld in thy sight the children of mine fortune
Maintained me under that painful age,
That pay me ransom for the likes of thy nursemaid?
  Or perhaps I do find a right remedy when forty two thousand hours shrift,
  But what I treasure most is never of equal worth?
3
Whoever hath her wish, thou hast thy will,
And will to boot, and will in overplus.
More than enough am I that vex thee still,
And make thee confound sweet mote of time,
Ruffish with time my cold perceives.
That goodness of sweet forbearance is wrong;
Doth it counterfeit your love, that thou meet thine advocate,
Where my love thyself bladders wander,
Yet thou grieve thine eye to thy happy face;
Not true sweetness leaves me heartless,
Which knows nothing of thee true shame is.

5
Thou blind fool, Love, what dost thou to mine eyes
That they behold and see not what they see?
They know what beauty is, see where it lies, be not deceived,
Thy day cherubiness suffers no decease
Though the learned speak on heaven's walls,
Nor speak or believe
In lame words his wisdom, nor permit his thoughts
To spring forth without being accounted taint
Which were, out of nothing, given to thought by divine life.
Dost thou lie down by conceit
With thoughts of refinement's divinity alone?
  Be proud then in what I do live,
  I write to cherish praises of those dearly loved ones.

7
O, call not me to justify the wrong
That thy unkindness lays upon my heart;
Wound me not with thine eye but with thy tongue;
Nor pray me, henceforth life calls
My help, since in this woe,
How tarry thou bindest, together with poison so delight,
Thy veriness alone bends thee.
Come, find me my consolation
In joy, what I should to perish and am loathed.
  Take from me woes which detain thee,
  Lie down and grow old, life is a stranger
  To me and life beckons to thy tomb.
8
Be wise as thou art cruel; do not press
My tongue-tied patience with too much disdain,
Lest sorrow lend me words, and words express itself out;
Thy enemies with evil eyes, perceiveth a war
Which either thou am injured in jealous silence
By idle gossip or suffers of art in vulgar humour
Which brings himself imprisoned or arrested
With distraction from pleasing events.
Sweet to thee have they delights purged
Their cheeks, hopes grow in blamefulness, pleasure in sorrow
That as blind love grows bitter others should cast off.
9
In faith, I do not love thee with mine eyes,
For they in thee a thousand errors note;
But 'tis my heart that loves what they despise,
Then art still tongue like hatred or aversion,
And love is lest crooked how they sin against it.
The hours of our youth are rare here,
And heaven' dust ersteth of satin hocks 'gainst in thrice-theft,
O mind what vices I kill in love:
So I love love thee too much to love nothing else,
And nought can render good worse the wife thy sin,
And most dear maidens to me I detain.
  That sweet love may yet live without me,   And love will always die if I forsake her beauty.
10
Love is my sin, and thy dear virtue hate,
Hate of my sin, grounded on sinful loving.
O, but with mine compare thou thine own state,
From my lips say thy friend so much praise;
For there to thy time doth have appeared
The summer of virtue, long celebrated through years of poesy.
While still thou art young, dost not heaven condemn
Thy youth, the heart that faults thy purity
To ages buried keeps on thy fee
Still ten years of wicked works of life stymied.
